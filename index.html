ArtManip : Category-Level Articulated In-Hand Manipulation

Learning dexterous policies of in-hand articulated objects that generalize across intra-class instances and initial grasps, with zero-shot sim2real capability.

Overview

What problem do we solve?

Articulated in-hand manipulation requires controlling an object's internal degree of freedom while stabilizing a free-floating base.

In-hand manipulation of articulated objects is challenging because articulation control is tightly coupled with grasp stability. Unlike environment-anchored articulated manipulation, the object base is free-floating in the hand; unlike rigid-object in-hand manipulation, the policy must actively actuate an internal joint while maintaining stable contacts.

We present a framework for category-level articulated in-hand manipulation under diverse initial grasp configurations. Our key idea is to automatically construct the training distribution through primitive-based articulated object generation and task-oriented functional grasp synthesis. Policies are trained in simulation with reward curriculum and teacher–student latent distillation, then deployed zero-shot to real objects using primitive-based digital twins.

Challenges

Why is this hard?

The difficulty is not only generating motion, but generating the right contacts, stabilizing the object, and handling uncertain articulation physics.

🧩

Articulated assets are hard to scale

Accurate models of small articulated tools are labor-intensive to reconstruct, annotate, and simulate at scale.

✋

Functional grasps are sensitive

The grasp must simultaneously stabilize the free-floating object and provide contacts that can actuate the joint.

⚙️

Joint physics vary across instances

Different damping, stiffness, friction, and mass distributions can drastically change the required finger behavior.

Method

Primitive assets → functional grasps → robust policy

The pipeline builds a scalable training distribution, then learns a deployable student policy under partial observations.

1

Primitive articulated assets

Generate two-link objects with one prismatic or revolute joint using simple box primitives.

2

Functional grasp synthesis

Use category-level contact-region templates to synthesize diverse task-oriented initial grasps.

3

Teacher policy learning

Train with privileged state, reward curriculum, and articulation physics randomization.

4

Student deployment

Distill a latent representation from observable history for zero-shot real-world execution.

Key Findings

What did we learn?

Three concise takeaways for the paper and presentation.

Primitive assets are enough for real transfer.

Task-relevant interaction structure matters more than high-fidelity visual geometry for this setting.

Curriculum resolves the progress–stability trade-off.

The policy must first learn to actuate the joint, then progressively enforce stable in-hand manipulation.

Functional grasps and physics randomization drive generalization.

High-quality contacts enable exploration; randomized articulation physics improves robustness.
